ShanghaiTech SIST Robotics Course Project

Teaching an xArm6 to fetch from two RGB views

We completed a real-robot imitation learning project for a tabletop task: a Gello leader arm was used to teleoperate an xArm6 follower, two RealSense RGB cameras recorded the scene, and an Action Chunking Transformer was trained and deployed to pick up a toy kettle and place it onto a toy stove.

Final project demo · June 21, 2026 Jinxi Xiao, Xuebing Li, Weile Li xArm6 + Gello + Dual RGB Completed ACT inference on hardware

A trained ACT policy runs on the xArm6 and executes the kettle-on-stove task from visual observations and robot proprioception.

Why this is hard

A small tabletop task still contains the hard parts of robot manipulation.

Pick-and-place is easy to describe but brittle on real hardware. The robot must localize the kettle, align the gripper, lift without slipping, transport smoothly, and release at the right place. A small error during grasping can become a large error during placement.

We deliberately use 2D RGB observations rather than depth or a full 3D reconstruction. This makes the setup cheaper and easier to deploy, while forcing the policy to learn useful visual and temporal cues from partial views, camera noise, and gripper occlusion.

50 expert demonstration episodes collected for the final dataset
2 Intel RealSense RGB cameras observing arm-side and front views
30Hz target recording frequency for synchronized robot-camera data
100 actions predicted in each ACT action chunk during policy rollout
20k training steps used for the ACT checkpoint schedule

How it works

One pipeline records human skill. The other turns it into a policy.

The data-collection loop reads the xArm state, grabs the latest RGB frames from both cameras, reads the Gello leader joint positions, sends the resulting command to the xArm follower, and writes the synchronized observation-action frame into the LeRobot dataset.

Offline training loaded the recorded dataset as action chunks. The ACT policy received the two-view image observation and robot proprioception, then learned to predict a short horizon of joint-space actions for physical rollout.

Human Gello leader arm produces teleoperation commands
Robot xArm6 follower executes joint and gripper targets
Vision Arm-side and front RGB cameras capture the workspace
Dataset LeRobot stores state, images, action, task, and time
Policy ACT executes chunked actions learned from demonstrations

Code project pipeline

The codebase turns teleoperation into an autonomous robot policy.

The implementation is built as a project-specific fork of LeRobot. It adds the Gello leader interface, the xArm6 follower interface, RealSense camera support, dataset recording, ACT training, and policy rollout into one reproducible robot-learning stack.

The important design choice is separation of responsibilities: hardware devices expose small interfaces, the recording loop only synchronizes observation and action streams, the dataset stores a fixed imitation-learning problem, and the policy layer consumes that dataset without touching hardware-specific details.

Whole Project Flow

From human demonstrations to real-robot inference

Step 1 Teleoperate

A human moves the Gello leader arm; seven Dynamixel readings represent six arm joints and the gripper.

Step 2 Record

The xArm6 executes the mapped command while robot state and two RGB camera streams are sampled at 30 Hz.

Step 3 Build Dataset

Each frame stores proprioception, cam-arm image, cam-front image, action, timestamp, and task label.

Step 4 Train ACT

The policy learns from 40 episodes and 14,320 frames, predicting 100-step action chunks.

Step 5 Roll Out

The trained checkpoint runs on the physical xArm6 and autonomously performs kettle placement.

Data Collection Flow

Human control, robot execution, visual sensing, and dataset writing happen in one loop.

Control xArm6 Follower

Training Flow

The recorded dataset becomes supervised action-chunk targets.

Dataset Episode-Aware Loading

Samples stay inside episode boundaries so action chunks do not cross resets or task endings.

Observation Image + State Tokens

Two RGB views are resized for the visual backbone and paired with the seven-dimensional robot state.

Encoder ResNet + Transformer

Visual features, proprioception, and latent information are fused into a shared representation.

Supervision Chunk Loss

The model reconstructs 100-step action sequences with an L1 term and VAE regularization.

Output Deployable Checkpoint

Saved policy weights, configuration, processor settings, and normalization statistics are used for rollout.

Inference Flow

The deployment loop mirrors recording, but actions come from the trained policy.

Observe Online Robot State

The xArm wrapper gathers calibrated joint state, gripper state, cam-arm RGB, and cam-front RGB.

Prepare Normalize Inputs

Preprocessors convert live observations into the same feature space used during training.

Predict ACT Action Chunk

The policy predicts a queue of future robot actions instead of making an isolated one-step decision.

Schedule Chunk Execution

An action interpolator and queue select the next command while preserving the target control rate.

Act xArm6 Motion

The robot executes joint and gripper targets, producing the autonomous pick-and-place demonstration.

Hardware Layer

Gello leader, xArm6 follower, gripper, and two RealSense cameras expose device-specific connect, observe, and act behavior.

Calibration Layer

Gello raw encoder readings are normalized into degrees, while xArm calibration maps between normalized commands and physical joint or gripper units.

Processor Layer

Observation and action processors keep the interface modular; in this project the mapping is mostly identity after calibration.

Dataset Layer

LeRobotDataset stores synchronized observation-action-task frames and preserves camera videos for later training, replay, and visualization.

Policy Layer

ACT uses a ResNet18 visual backbone, Transformer encoder-decoder, VAE latent variable, and 100-step action chunk output.

Rollout Layer

The deployment strategy repeatedly observes, preprocesses, predicts, schedules, and sends actions until the task is complete.

What the dataset contains

  • Seven-dimensional robot state: six xArm joints plus gripper position.
  • Two synchronized RGB observations: an arm-side camera and a front scene camera.
  • Seven-dimensional action targets produced by the Gello leader and executed by the xArm follower.
  • A natural-language task label, “put kettle on stove,” stored with every demonstration frame.
  • Episode-level organization that supports replay, visualization, training, and closed-loop debugging.

Why the implementation is robust enough for hardware

  • Camera devices are selected by RealSense serial number, so cam-arm and cam-front do not swap accidentally.
  • Background camera reading keeps the control loop close to 30 Hz instead of blocking on slow image capture.
  • xArm safety limits can cap relative target motion, reducing the risk of sudden large commands.
  • Streaming video encoding prevents episode saving from becoming the slowest part of data collection.
  • Rollout uses the same observation schema as recording, which reduces train-deploy mismatch.

ACT-specific details

  • The default model uses a ResNet18 image backbone with ImageNet initialization.
  • Multiple camera views are treated as separate visual streams before being fused by the Transformer.
  • The model predicts a 100-step action chunk, matching the temporal abstraction used in ACT.
  • The VAE encoder is used during training to model demonstration action sequences; inference uses the policy decoder.
  • History-frame support allows the model to receive the current frame plus recent observations when the history window is enabled.

Engineering lessons from the completed project

  • The most important integration point is consistent calibration between Gello degrees and xArm physical joint units.
  • Dataset quality depends on synchronized sensing, not only on whether the robot visibly completes the task.
  • Joint-space control is a practical choice for this setup because it maps cleanly to the xArm follower interface.
  • The code structure keeps data collection, training, and deployment separable, making failures easier to diagnose.
  • The final inference video demonstrates the whole pipeline rather than a single isolated model component.

ACT policy

Instead of predicting one action, the model predicts a short trajectory.

Action Chunking Transformers reduce the short-sightedness of step-by-step behavioral cloning. At every policy query, the network predicts a sequence of actions, and temporal ensembling can blend overlapping predictions into smoother execution.

In our implementation, the observation includes two RGB images and the xArm proprioceptive state. The action target is a chunk of robot commands. During training, the CVAE encoder models the demonstration action sequence through a latent style variable. At deployment time, the decoder acts as the policy.

What we learned

A compact real-robot setup was enough to complete the full learning loop.

The project started from a focused manipulation problem and ended as a complete real-hardware demonstration. Along the way, the system forced us to solve the same practical issues that appear in larger robot-learning pipelines: hardware synchronization, camera calibration, action representation, dataset quality, and temporal consistency.

The technical principles from the report explain why the demo works: ACT converts teleoperated behavior into action chunks, the dual RGB cameras provide complementary visual grounding, and the LeRobot dataset format keeps observations, actions, and task labels aligned for supervised imitation learning.

Two-view perception

The arm-side camera captures gripper-object interaction, while the front camera gives a wider scene-level view of the kettle and stove.

Stable joint-space control

Joint-space commands match the xArm follower interface and avoid adding an extra inverse-kinematics layer inside the learned policy.

Hardware-first validation

The final deliverable is not only a training pipeline, but a visible inference video showing the learned policy acting on the real robot.
